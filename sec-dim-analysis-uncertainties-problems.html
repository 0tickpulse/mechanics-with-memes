    Exercises 1.5 Additional Problems

    1. Up.

    How many helium balloons would it take to lift a house of mass \(100,000 \, \text{kg}\) off the ground?
    🔗
    Data:
    • The density of helium is approximately \(0.18 \, \text{kg/m}^3\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The density of air is approximately \(1.2 \, \text{kg/m}^3\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • A standard spherical party balloon has a diameter of about 0.3 meters, so its volume is approximately \(0.014 \, \text{m}^3\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    According to Archimedes’ principle, the buoyant force on a balloon is equal to the weight of the air it displaces minus the weight of the helium inside it. The weight of something is given by its mass times \(g \approx 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    For a single balloon, the buoyant force is given by
    \begin{align*} F_b \amp = m_{\text{air}} g - m_{\text{helium}} g \\ \amp = (\rho_{\text{air}} V - \rho_{\text{helium}} V) g \\ \amp = (\rho_{\text{air}} - \rho_{\text{helium}}) V g \text{,} \end{align*}
    where \(\rho_{\text{air}}\) and \(\rho_{\text{helium}}\) are the densities of air and helium, respectively, \(V\) is the volume of the balloon, and \(g\) is the acceleration due to gravity.
    🔗
    Plugging in the numbers, we get
    \begin{align*} F_b \amp = (1.2 \, \text{kg/m}^3 - 0.18 \, \text{kg/m}^3) (0.014 \, \text{m}^3) (9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2) \\ \amp \approx 0.15 \, \text{N} \text{.} \end{align*}
    To lift a house of mass \(100,000 \, \text{kg}\text{,}\) we need the buoyant force to overcome the weight of the house, which is given by
    \begin{align*} F_\text{house} \amp = m_\text{house} g \\ \amp = (100,000 \, \text{kg}) (9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2) \\ \amp = 980,000 \, \text{N} \text{.} \end{align*}
    Therefore, the number of balloons needed is given by
    \begin{align*} N \amp = \frac{F_\text{house}}{F_b} \\ \amp = \frac{980,000 \, \text{N}}{0.15 \, \text{N}} \\ \amp \approx 6.5 \times 10^6 \text{.} \end{align*}
    So it would take approximately 6.5 million helium balloons to lift the house off the ground. Indeed, Carl Fredricksen’s house in the movie Up would not be able to fly with just a few hundred balloons!
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Exercise Group.

    For this set of problems, you will need the following data:
    • The atmosphere has a mass of approximately \(5.15 \times 10^{18} \, \text{kg}\text{.}\) Its average molar mass is about \(29 \, \text{g} \cdot \text{mol}^{-1}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The average human breathes about \(0.5 \, \text{L}\) of air per breath.
      🔗
      🔗
    • At room temperature and atmospheric pressure, one mole of an ideal gas occupies about \(22.4 \, \text{L} \cdot \text{mol}^{-1}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • A person takes about 15 breaths per minute.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    2.

    How many molecules does a person breathe each year? How many in 20 years?
    🔗
    Solution.
    Before doing anything, let’s convert all the data to the dimension of molecules.
    • The number of moles in the atmosphere is given by
      \begin{equation*} \frac{5.15 \times 10^{18} \, \text{kg}}{29 \, \text{g} \cdot \text{mol}^{-1}} = \frac{5.15 \times 10^{21} \, \text{g}}{29 \, \text{g} \cdot \text{mol}^{-1}} \approx 1.78 \times 10^{20} \, \text{mol}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Therefore, the number of molecules in the atmosphere is given by
      \begin{equation*} (1.78 \times 10^{20} \, \text{mol})(6.022 \times 10^{23} \, \text{mol}^{-1}) = 10^{44}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • The number of molecules in a single breath can be determined by the molar volume as given above. The number of moles in a single breath is given by
      \begin{equation*} n_\text{breath} = \frac{0.5 \, \text{L}}{22.4 \, \text{L} \cdot \text{mol}^{-1}} \approx 0.0223 \, \text{mol}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Thus, the number of molecules in a single breath is given by
    \begin{equation*} N_\text{breath} = (0.0223 \, \text{mol})(6.022 \times 10^{23} \, \text{mol}^{-1}) \approx 10^{22}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Assuming we take about 15 breaths per minute, we take about \(15 \times 60 \times 24 \times 365 \approx 7.9 \times 10^6\) breaths per year. Therefore, the number of molecules we breathe in a year is given by
    \begin{equation*} N_\text{year} = (7.9 \times 10^6)(10^{22}) = 7.9 \times 10^{28}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In 20 years, we would breathe in about \(20( 7.9 \times 10^{28}) = 1.6 \times 10^{30}\) molecules.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    What is the probability that a single molecule from the atmosphere has not been in my lungs in the last 20 years?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The total number of molecules in the atmosphere is approximately \(10^{44}\text{,}\) and the number of molecules we breathe in 20 years is approximately \(1.6 \times 10^{30}\text{.}\) Therefore, the probability that a single molecule from the atmosphere has not been in my lungs in the last 20 years is given by
    \begin{equation*} P = 1 - \frac{1.6 \times 10^{30}}{10^{44}} = 1 - 1.6 \times 10^{-14} \approx 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So the probability is essentially 1, meaning that it is almost certain that a given molecule from the atmosphere has not been in my lungs in the last 20 years. There’s a caveat, though!
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    What is the probability that you are currently breathing in a molecule that was in my lungs at some point in the last 20 years?
    🔗
    Hint.
    First find the probability that zero of the molecules in your current breath were in my lungs in the last 20 years, and then subtract that from 1 to get the probability that at least one molecule in your current breath was in my lungs.
    🔗
    The probability will be incredibly difficult to compute, even with a calculator, so you will need to use some approximations. Use the fact that
    \begin{equation*} e^{-x} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \qty(1 - \frac{x}{n})^n \end{equation*}
    to your advantage.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    The probability that zero of the molecules in your current breath were in my lungs in the last 20 years is given by
    \begin{equation*} P_0 = \qty(1 - \frac{1.6 \times 10^{30}}{10^{44}})^{10^{22}} \approx e^{-\frac{1.6 \times 10^{30}}{10^{44}} \times 10^{22}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This can be approximated by
    \begin{equation*} P_0 = \qty(1 - \frac{1.6 \times 10^{8}}{10^{22}})^{10^{22}} \approx e^{-1.6 \times 10^{8}} \approx 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Thus, the probability that at least one molecule in your current breath was in my lungs at some point in the last 20 years is given by
    \begin{equation*} P = 1 - P_0 \approx 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So the probability is essentially 1, meaning that it is almost certain that you are currently breathing in a molecule that was in my lungs at some point in the last 20 years.
    🔗
    In other words, you are taking my breath away as you read this sentence.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    5.

    Generalize this approach. Suppose an event has a probability of \(p\) of occurring in a single trial, and we have \(n\) independent trials, where \(n\) is unbelievably large. What is the probability that the event occurs at least once in those \(n\) trials?
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(q = 1 - p\) be the probability that the event does not occur in a single trial. The probability that the event does not occur in any of the \(n\) trials is given by
    \begin{equation*} P_0 = q^n = (1 - p)^n \approx e^{-pn}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the probability that the event occurs at least once in those \(n\) trials is given by
    \begin{equation*} P = 1 - P_0 \approx 1 - e^{-pn}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    6.

    Hence, determine the probability that the event occurs \(k\) times in those \(n\) trials, where \(k \lt n\) is a fixed positive integer. Use the approximation
    \begin{equation*} \binom{n}{k} \approx \frac{n^k}{k!} \end{equation*}
    for large \(n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The probability that the event occurs exactly \(k\) times in those \(n\) trials is given by the binomial distribution:
    \begin{equation*} P_k = \binom{n}{k} p^k q^{n-k} = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1 - p)^{n-k}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Let’s take the limit as \(n \to \infty\) while keeping \(k\) fixed. Therefore, we have
    \begin{equation*} P_k \approx \frac{n^k}{k!} p^k (1 - p)^{n-k} \approx \frac{(pn)^k}{k!} e^{-pn}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This, by the way, is called the Poisson distribution.
    🔗

    7.

    Astronomers use light to determine the properties of distant objects in the universe, such as their mass, size, and composition. To separate light into its component wavelengths, a diffraction grating — a device with many closely spaced slits — is used. The diffraction grating causes the light to interfere constructively at certain angles as given by
    \begin{equation*} d \sin \theta = m \lambda\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(d\) is the distance between the slits, \(\theta\) is the angle of the diffracted light, \(m\) is an integer called the order of the diffraction, and \(\lambda\) is the wavelength of the light.
    🔗
    In a test of a new diffraction grating, an astronomer shines light of wavelength \(500 \pm 10 \, \text{nm}\) onto the grating and measures the angle of the first-order diffraction (\(m = 1\)) to be \(30^\circ \pm 1^\circ\text{.}\) Determine the relative uncertainty in the distance between the slits \(d\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    The uncertainty in \(\sin \theta\) is
    \begin{equation*} \Delta (\sin \theta) = |\cos \theta| \Delta \theta\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which can be determined using differential calculus (don’t worry if you haven’t learned calculus yet.).
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    We can rearrange the diffraction equation to solve for \(d\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} d = \frac{m \lambda}{\sin \theta}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The relative uncertainty in \(d\) can be found by combining the relative uncertainties in \(\lambda\) and \(\sin \theta\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\Delta d}{d} = \sqrt{\qty(\frac{\Delta \lambda}{\lambda})^2 + \qty(\frac{\Delta (\sin \theta)}{\sin \theta})^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    As given in the hint, this is
    \begin{align*} \frac{\Delta d}{d} \amp = \sqrt{\qty(\frac{\Delta \lambda}{\lambda})^2 + \qty(\frac{|\cos \theta| \Delta \theta}{\sin \theta})^2}\\ \amp = \sqrt{\qty(\frac{\Delta \lambda}{\lambda})^2 + \qty(\frac{1}{\tan \theta} \Delta \theta)^2}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Thus
    \begin{align*} \frac{\Delta d}{d} \amp = \sqrt{\qty(\frac{10 \times 10^{-9}}{500 \times 10^{-9}})^2 + \qty(\frac{1}{\tan 30^\circ} \cdot \frac{\pi}{180})^2}\\ \amp \approx 0.036 \text{.} \end{align*}
