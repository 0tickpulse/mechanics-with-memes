    Section 3.2 Differential Equation Workshop

    In the previous section, we outlined Newton’s three laws of motion. Part of moving the bronze statue involves applying a force to it, and Newton’s second law gives us a mathematical relationship between the force we apply and the resulting motion of the statue. But we will need a set of tools to solve this mathematical relationship.
    🔗

    Subsection 3.2.1 Classification of Differential Equations

    Let’s first discuss how to classify the types of differential equations we will encounter in this chapter. As we saw in the previous section, differential equations are equations that relate a function to its derivatives. We have to solve for the function to find the motion of the statue. Since our equation of motion always has the form \(F = m \dv[2]{x}{t}\text{,}\) it will always have a second derivative. A differential equation whose highest derivative is a second derivative is called a second-order differential equation.
    🔗
    There’s one trick we can pull, however, to make our lives easier. Namely, if the force doesn’t depend on the position of the statue, then we can rewrite the equation of motion as \(F = m \dv{v}{t}\text{,}\) where \(v = \dv{x}{t}\) is the velocity of the statue. Then, this is just a first-order differential equation, which is easier to solve than a second-order one. This section will be devoted to solving first-order differential equations.
    🔗
    Also, the differential equations we will encounter in this chapter will be ordinary differential equations (ODEs), which means that the function we are solving for is a function of a single variable (in our case, time). This is in contrast to partial differential equations (PDEs), which involve functions of multiple variables and their partial derivatives. (For example, perhaps we have a PDE that describes the temperature distribution in the van as a function of both time and position, and we want to solve for the temperature at a particular point in the van as a function of time.)
    🔗
    The degree of a differential equation is the highest power of the highest derivative that appears in the equation. The equation
    \begin{equation*} \dv{v}{t} = -\frac{\beta}{m} v^2 \end{equation*}
    is a first-order, second-degree differential equation, since the highest derivative is a first derivative and the highest power of that derivative is 2.
    🔗
    A differential equation is called linear if the function and its derivatives appear to the first power and are not multiplied together. The equation
    \begin{equation*} \dv[2]{x}{t} + 3 \sin(t) \dv{x}{t} + 2 x = \cos(t) \end{equation*}
    is linear, whereas
    \begin{equation*} \dv[2]{x}{t} + 3 \sin(t) \qty(\dv{x}{t})^2 + 2 x \dv{x}{t} = \cos(t) \end{equation*}
    is not linear due to the \(\dv{x}{t}^2\) and \(x \dv{x}{t}\) terms.
    🔗
    A differential equation is called homogeneous if all the terms involve the function or its derivatives, and there are no terms that are just functions of the independent variable. The equation
    \begin{equation*} \dv[2]{x}{t} + 3 \sin(t) \dv{x}{t} + 2 x = 0 \end{equation*}
    is homogeneous, whereas
    \begin{equation*} \dv[2]{x}{t} + 3 \sin(t) \dv{x}{t} + 2 x = \cos(t) \end{equation*}
    is not homogeneous due to the \(\cos(t)\) term on the right-hand side.
    🔗
    Finally, a differential equation is autonomous if the independent variable (in our case, time) does not explicitly appear in the equation. The equation
    \begin{equation*} \dv{v}{t} = av^2 \end{equation*}
    is autonomous, since time does not explicitly appear in the equation, whereas
    \begin{equation*} \dv{v}{t} = av^2 + b\sin(t) \end{equation*}
    is not autonomous due to the \(b\sin(t)\) term on the right-hand side.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.2.2 Separable Differential Equations (and Air Resistance)

    The simplest equation of motion is that of a constant force, which is the case when we push the statue with a constant force and there are no other forces acting on it (like friction or air resistance).
    🔗

    Example 3.2.1.

    The team is using a constant-force winch to pull the statue across a frictionless floor (they’ve applied plenty of industrial grease). The magnitude of the force applied by the winch is \(F_0\text{,}\) and the statue has a mass of \(m\text{.}\) What is the velocity of the statue as a function of time?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The equation of motion is \(F_0 = m \dv{v}{t}\text{.}\) Solving this involves just integrating both sides with respect to time:
    \begin{align*} \amp \amp \dv{v}{t} \amp = \frac{F_0}{m}\\ \implies \amp \amp \int \dv{v}{t} \dd{t} \amp = \int \frac{F_0}{m} \dd{t}\\ \implies \amp \amp v \amp = \frac{F_0}{m} t + C\text{.} \end{align*}
    Next, we can determine the constant of integration \(C\) by using the initial condition that the statue starts at rest, so \(v(0) = 0\text{.}\) When we plug in \(t = 0\text{,}\) we get \(0 = \frac{F_0}{m} \cdot 0 + C\text{,}\) so \(C = 0\text{.}\) Therefore, the velocity of the statue as a function of time is \(v(t) = \frac{F_0}{m} t\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    As the example shows, the velocity of the statue increases linearly with time when a constant force is applied. Generally, when the derivative is constant, we can just integrate to find the function, and the solution will be a linear function of time.
    🔗
    What if the force isn’t constant? One specific type of differential equation that we can solve is a separable differential equation, which looks like \(\dv{v}{t} = f(v) g(t)\text{,}\) where the right-hand side is a product of a function of \(v\) and a function of \(t\text{.}\) (Notice by the way, this type of equation is always homogeneous.)
    🔗
    There’s a nice example of a separable differential equation that comes up in the context of air resistance. When a solid (the van) travels through a fluid (the air), it experiences a drag force, which is a force that opposes the motion of the object. Generally, the exact form of the drag force is very complicated, and it depends on the shape of the object, the properties of the fluid, and the speed of the object.
    🔗
    For a solid traveling through air at high speed, the drag force is approximately proportional to (1) the density \(\rho\) of the air, (2) the cross-sectional area \(A\) of the object, and (3) the square of the speed of the object, \(v^2\text{.}\) Therefore, we can write the magnitude of the drag force as \(F_\text{drag} \approx \frac{1}{2} \rho A C_d v^2\text{.}\) The constant \(C_d\) is called the drag coefficient, and it depends on the shape of the object.
    🔗

    Example 3.2.2.

    The van is coasting at high speed, but Dave shifts into neutral, so the engine is no longer providing any force to propel the van forward. Right now, the van is moving at an initial velocity of \(v_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    They are being slowed down by air resistance, which has a drag coefficient of \(C_d\text{.}\) The density of air is \(\rho\text{,}\) and the cross-sectional area of the van is \(A\text{.}\) What is the velocity of the van as a function of time?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The equation of motion is \(m \dv{v}{t} = -\frac{1}{2} \rho A C_d v^2\text{.}\) For the sake of simplicity, let’s define \(\beta = \frac{1}{2} \rho A C_d\text{,}\) so the equation of motion becomes \(m \dv{v}{t} = -\beta v^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    This is a separable differential equation, since we can rewrite it as \(\dv{v}{t} = -\frac{\beta}{m} v^2\text{,}\) which is of the form \(\dv{v}{t} = f(v) g(t)\) with \(f(v) = -\frac{\beta}{m} v^2\) and \(g(t) = 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The strategy for solving a separable differential equation is to, as the name suggests, place all the \(v\)’s on one side and all the \(t\)’s on the other side, and then integrate both sides. We can rewrite the equation as
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{v^2} \dd{v} = -\frac{\beta}{m} \dd{t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now we can integrate both sides:
    \begin{align*} \amp \amp \int \frac{1}{v^2} \dd{v} \amp = \int -\frac{\beta}{m} \dd{t}\\ \implies \amp \amp -\frac{1}{v} \amp = -\frac{\beta}{m} t + C\text{.} \end{align*}
    Next, we can determine the constant of integration \(C\) by using the initial condition that the van starts with an initial velocity of \(v_0\text{,}\) so \(v(0) = v_0\text{.}\) When we plug in \(t = 0\text{,}\) we get \(-\frac{1}{v_0} = -\frac{\beta}{m} \cdot 0 + C\text{,}\) so \(C = -\frac{1}{v_0}\text{.}\) Therefore, the velocity of the van as a function of time is
    \begin{equation*} v(t) = \frac{1}{\frac{\beta}{m} t + \frac{1}{v_0}} = \frac{v_0 m}{\beta v_0 t + m}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Let’s generalize this result to the case where we have a general separable first-order ordinary differential equation of the form \(\dv{v}{t} = f(v) g(t)\text{.}\) We can rewrite this as \(\frac{1}{f(v)} \dd{v} = g(t) \dd{t}\text{.}\) Now we can integrate both sides:
    \begin{align*} \amp \amp \int \frac{1}{f(v)} \dd{v} \amp = \int g(t) \dd{t}\\ \implies \amp \amp \int \frac{1}{f(v)} \dd{v} \amp = \int g(t) \dd{t} + C\text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗

    Definition 3.2.3.

    A separable first-order ordinary differential equation is a first-order ordinary differential equation of the form
    \begin{equation*} \dv{v}{t} = f(v) g(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The general solution to a separable first-order ordinary differential equation is given by
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{1}{f(v)} \dd{v} = \int g(t) \dd{t} + C\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(C\) is a constant of integration.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.2.3 Linear Inhomogeneous Differential Equations

    The next type of differential equation we will solve is a linear inhomogeneous differential equation, which has the form
    \begin{equation*} \dv{v}{t} + p(t) v = q(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The functions \(p(t)\) and \(q(t)\) are given functions of time, and we are solving for the function \(v(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The general method to solving this type of equation is to multiply both sides by the integrating factor, which is given by
    \begin{equation*} \mu(t) = e^{\int p(t) \dd{t}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The reason we are interested in the integrating factor is that its derivative is just
    \begin{equation*} \dv{\mu}{t} = p(t) e^{\int p(t) \dd{t}} = p(t) \mu(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus, if we multiply both sides of the equation by the integrating factor, we get
    \begin{align*} \amp \amp \mu(t) \dv{v}{t} + \mu(t) p(t) v \amp = \mu(t) q(t)\\ \implies \amp \amp \mu(t) \dv{v}{t} + v \dv{\mu}{t} \amp = \mu(t) q(t)\\ \implies \amp \amp \dv{}{t} \qty(\mu(t) v) \amp = \mu(t) q(t)\text{.} \end{align*}
    Now we can integrate both sides:
    \begin{align*} \amp \amp \int \dv{}{t} \qty(\mu(t) v) \dd{t} \amp = \int \mu(t) q(t) \dd{t}\\ \implies \amp \amp \mu(t) v \amp = \int \mu(t) q(t) \dd{t} + C\text{.} \end{align*}
    Finally, we can solve for \(v(t)\) by dividing both sides by the integrating factor:
    \begin{equation*} v(t) = \frac{1}{\mu(t)} \qty(\int \mu(t) q(t) \dd{t} + C)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 3.2.4.

    Part of the scouting mission involved swimming across a river, and Dave is now wondering how long it would take to swim across the river.
    🔗
    Fluids exert a drag force on objects moving through them. Their viscosity is a measure of how much drag force they exert. At low speeds, the drag force is approximately proportional to the velocity of the object, so we can write the magnitude of the drag force as \(F_\text{drag} = 6 \pi \eta r v\) (known as Stokes’ law), where \(\eta\) is the viscosity of the fluid, \(r\) is the radius of the object, and \(v\) is the speed of the object.
    🔗
    If Dave swims with a constant force of magnitude \(F_0\text{,}\) then the equation of motion is
    \begin{equation*} m \dv{v}{t} = F_0 - 6 \pi \eta r v\text{.} \end{equation*}
    What is the velocity of Dave as a function of time, and how long does it take for Dave to swim across the river, which has a width of \(d\text{?}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The equation of motion can be rewritten as
    \begin{equation*} \dv{v}{t} + \frac{6 \pi \eta r}{m} v = \frac{F_0}{m}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which is a linear inhomogeneous differential equation with \(p(t) = \frac{6 \pi \eta r}{m}\) and \(q(t) = \frac{F_0}{m}\text{.}\) Let’s define \(\gamma = 6 \pi \eta r /m\) for the sake of simplicity, so the equation becomes
    \begin{equation*} \dv{v}{t} + \gamma v = \frac{F_0}{m}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The integrating factor is
    \begin{equation*} \mu(t) = e^{\int \gamma \dd{t}} = e^{\gamma t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Multiplying both sides of the equation by the integrating factor, we get
    \begin{align*} \amp \amp e^{\gamma t} \dv{v}{t} + e^{\gamma t} \gamma v \amp = e^{\gamma t} \frac{F_0}{m}\\ \implies \amp \amp \dv{}{t} \qty(e^{\gamma t} v) \amp = e^{\gamma t} \frac{F_0}{m}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Now we can integrate both sides:
    \begin{align*} \amp \amp \int \dv{}{t} \qty(e^{\gamma t} v) \dd{t} \amp = \int e^{\gamma t} \frac{F_0}{m} \dd{t}\\ \implies \amp \amp e^{\gamma t} v \amp = \frac{F_0}{m} \int e^{\gamma t} \dd{t} + C\\ \amp \amp \amp = \frac{F_0}{m} \cdot \frac{1}{\gamma} e^{\gamma t} + C\\ \implies \amp \amp v(t) \amp = \frac{F_0}{\gamma} + C e^{-\gamma t}\text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Notice, by the way, that this equation was technically also separable. The next one, however, is not.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.2.5.

    The force Dave applies to swim across the river may be more accurately modeled as a force that oscillates in time, such as \(F(t) = F_0 \sin(\omega t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solve the equation of motion
    \begin{equation*} m \dv{v}{t} = F_0 \sin(\omega t) - 6 \pi \eta r v \end{equation*}
    for the velocity of Dave as a function of time.
    🔗
    Then show that if Dave starts at rest, the velocity of Dave as a function of time is
    \begin{equation*} v(t) = \frac{F_0}{m (\gamma^2 + \omega^2)} \qty(-\gamma \sin(\omega t) + \omega (e^{-\gamma t} - \cos(\omega t)))\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\gamma = 6 \pi \eta r /m\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Hint 1.
    The integral
    \begin{equation*} \int e^{\gamma t} \sin(\omega t) \dd{t} \end{equation*}
    can be evaluated using integration by parts twice.
    🔗
    🔗
    Hint 2.
    The integral is equal to
    \begin{equation*} \int e^{\gamma t} \sin(\omega t) \dd{t} = -\frac{e^{\gamma t}}{\gamma^2 + \omega^2} (\gamma \sin(\omega t) + \omega \cos(\omega t)) + C\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    The equation of motion can be rewritten as
    \begin{equation*} \dv{v}{t} + \frac{6 \pi \eta r}{m} v = \frac{F_0}{m} \sin(\omega t)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which is a linear inhomogeneous differential equation with \(p(t) = \frac{6 \pi \eta r}{m}\) and \(q(t) = \frac{F_0}{m} \sin(\omega t)\text{.}\) Let’s define \(\gamma = 6 \pi \eta r /m\) for the sake of simplicity, so the equation becomes
    \begin{equation*} \dv{v}{t} + \gamma v = \frac{F_0}{m} \sin(\omega t)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The integrating factor is
    \begin{equation*} \mu(t) = e^{\int \gamma \dd{t}} = e^{\gamma t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Multiplying both sides of the equation by the integrating factor, we get
    \begin{align*} \amp \amp e^{\gamma t} \dv{v}{t} + e^{\gamma t} \gamma v \amp = e^{\gamma t} \frac{F_0}{m} \sin(\omega t)\\ \implies \amp \amp \dv{}{t} \qty(e^{\gamma t} v) \amp = e^{\gamma t} \frac{F_0}{m} \sin(\omega t)\text{.} \end{align*}
    Now we can integrate both sides:
    \begin{align*} \amp \amp \int \dv{}{t} \qty(e^{\gamma t} v) \dd{t} \amp = \int e^{\gamma t} \frac{F_0}{m} \sin(\omega t) \dd{t}\\ \implies \amp \amp e^{\gamma t} v \amp = -\frac{F_0}{m (\gamma^2 + \omega^2)} e^{\gamma t} (\gamma \sin(\omega t) + \omega \cos(\omega t)) + C\\ \implies \amp \amp v(t) \amp = -\frac{F_0}{m (\gamma^2 + \omega^2)} (\gamma \sin(\omega t) + \omega \cos(\omega t)) + C e^{-\gamma t}\text{.} \end{align*}
    We can determine the constant of integration \(C\) by using the initial condition that Dave starts at rest, so \(v(0) = 0\text{.}\) When we plug in \(t = 0\text{,}\) we get
    \begin{equation*} 0 = -\frac{F_0}{m (\gamma^2 + \omega^2)} \omega + C\text{,} \end{equation*}
    so \(C = \frac{F_0 \omega}{m (\gamma^2 + \omega^2)}\text{.}\) Therefore, the velocity of Dave as a function of time is
    \begin{equation*} v(t) = \frac{F_0}{m (\gamma^2 + \omega^2)} \qty(-\gamma \sin(\omega t) + \omega (e^{-\gamma t} - \cos(\omega t)))\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We can, in fact, combine the sine and cosine terms into a single sine term with a phase shift
    \begin{equation*} v(t) = \frac{F_0}{m \sqrt{\gamma^2 + \omega^2}} \qty(e^{-\gamma t} - \sin(\omega t + \phi))\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\phi = \tan^{-1}(\gamma / \omega)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.2.4 Homogeneous Differential Equations

    Another type of differential equation we will encounter is a homogeneous differential equation, which has the form
    \begin{equation*} \dv{v}{t} = f\qty(\frac{v}{t})\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The function on the right-hand side is a function of the ratio \(v/t\text{.}\)
    🔗
    This type of equation can be solved by making the substitution \(z = v/t\text{,}\) which implies that \(v = tz\text{.}\) Then, by the product rule of differentiation, we have
    \begin{equation*} \dv{v}{t} = z + t \dv{z}{t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Substituting this into the original equation, we get
    \begin{equation*} z + t \dv{z}{t} = f(z)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is separable, leading to
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{f(z) - z} \dd{z} = \frac{1}{t} \dd{t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 3.2.6.

    Suppose that the force Dave applies to swim across the river is proportional to the ratio of his velocity to the time he’s been swimming, so \(F(t) = k \frac{v}{t}\text{.}\) (Perhaps Dave is getting more tired as time goes on, so the force he can apply decreases with time, but it also increases with his velocity since he’s a bit of a speed demon.) Ignoring the viscous force for now, determine the equation of motion for Dave, and solve for the velocity of Dave as a function of time. At what velocity is this model valid?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The equation of motion is
    \begin{equation*} m \dv{v}{t} = k \frac{v}{t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is a homogeneous differential equation, since the right-hand side is a function of the ratio \(v/t\text{.}\) We can solve this by making the substitution \(z = v/t\text{,}\) which implies that \(v = tz\text{.}\) Then, by the product rule of differentiation, we have
    \begin{equation*} \dv{v}{t} = z + t \dv{z}{t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Substituting this into the original equation, we get
    \begin{equation*} z + t \dv{z}{t} = \frac{k}{m} z\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Rearranging gives
    \begin{equation*} t \dv{z}{t} = \qty(\frac{k}{m} - 1) z\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Let \(\alpha = \frac{k}{m} - 1\) for the sake of simplicity, so the equation becomes
    \begin{equation*} t \dv{z}{t} = \alpha z\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is separable, leading to
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{z} \dd{z} = \frac{\alpha}{t} \dd{t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now we can integrate both sides:
    \begin{align*} \amp \amp \int \frac{1}{z} \dd{z} \amp = \int \frac{\alpha}{t} \dd{t}\\ \implies \amp \amp \ln|z| \amp = \alpha \ln|t| + C\\ \implies \amp \amp z \amp = C' t^\alpha\text{.} \end{align*}
    Finally, we can substitute back to get
    \begin{equation*} v(t) = C' t^{\alpha + 1} = C' t^{\frac{k}{m}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This model is valid when the viscous force is negligible compared to the force Dave applies. This is only the case when \(6 \pi \eta r v \ll k \frac{v}{t}\text{,}\) which implies that \(t \ll \frac{k}{6 \pi \eta r}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 3.2.5 Bernoulli Differential Equations

    The last type of first-order differential equation we will encounter (for now) is a Bernoulli differential equation, which has the form
    \begin{equation*} \dv{v}{t} + p(t) v = q(t) v^n\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(n\) is a real number. This is a nonlinear differential equation, since the function \(v\) appears to the power of \(n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    However, with a clever substitution of \(u = v^{1-n}\text{,}\) we can turn this into a linear differential equation. The derivative of \(u\) with respect to \(t\) is
    \begin{equation*} \dv{u}{t} = \dv{u}{v} \dv{v}{t} = (1-n) v^{-n} \dv{v}{t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Multiply the original equation by \(v^{-n}\) to get
    \begin{equation*} v^{-n} \dv{v}{t} + p(t) v^{1-n} = q(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Notice that the left-hand side is just \(\frac{1}{1-n} \dv{u}{t} + p(t) u\text{.}\) Therefore, we can rewrite the equation as
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{1-n} \dv{u}{t} + p(t) u = q(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Multiplying both sides by \(1-n\) gives
    \begin{equation*} \dv{u}{t} + (1-n) p(t) u = (1-n) q(t)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which is a linear differential equation that we can solve using the integrating factor method as described above.
    🔗

    Example 3.2.7.

    After the van has stopped, the headlights stopped, and are cooling down. The surrounding air has a temperature of \(T_\text{air}\text{,}\) and the headlights have a temperature of \(T(t)\) at time \(t\text{.}\) This function can be modeled by the equation
    \begin{equation*} \dv{T}{t} + aT = bT^2\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(a\) and \(b\) are positive constants that depend on the properties of the headlights and the surrounding air.
