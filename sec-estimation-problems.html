    Section 1.3 Estimation Problems

    Before the team spends $500 on a heavy-duty winch or continues with the heist plan, they need to make some sanity checks. We don’t need to know the exact volume of the statue to the third decimal place yet — we just need to know if we’re dealing with the weight of a motorcycle or the weight of an elephant. This is the art of the Fermi Problem: making a calculated guess when you have limited data.
    🔗

    Subsection 1.3.1 Orders of Magnitude

    An order of magnitude is a way of expressing the scale of a quantity in terms of powers of ten. When we express a quantity in scientific notation, the exponent of ten gives us the order of magnitude of the quantity.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 1.3.1.

    Determine the order of magnitude of the following quantities:
    1. The mass of the Earth (look it up) in kilograms.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(324,000 \, \text{nm}\text{.}\) Convert this to meters first!
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Avogadro’s number (\(6.022 \times 10^{23}\)) squared. Try this without a calculator.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. The mass of the Earth is approximately \(5.972 \times 10^{24} \, \text{kg}\text{,}\) so its order of magnitude is 24.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(324,000 \, \text{nm} = 324,000 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{m} = 3.24 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{m}\text{,}\) so its order of magnitude is -4.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Avogadro’s number squared is \((6.022 \times 10^{23})^2 \approx 36 \times 10^{46} = 3.6 \times 10^{47}\text{,}\) so its order of magnitude is 47.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In a Fermi problem, we attempt to estimate a quantity by breaking it down into smaller, more manageable pieces and making reasonable assumptions about each piece. Our goal is to get an estimate that is within an order of magnitude of the true value, which is often good enough for making decisions. Being off by a factor of 2, for example, is completely fine in a Fermi problem.
    🔗
    "How do we know it’s 500 kg?" Sarah asked. "The rival school’s website just says it’s ’life-sized bronze’."
    🔗
    Dave grabbed a napkin. "Let’s estimate. Richard Feynman was about 1.75 meters tall. Let’s model him as a cylinder, roughly 0.5 meters wide and 0.2 meters deep."
    🔗
    Dave uses the formula for the volume of an elliptic cylinder, \(V = \pi abh\text{,}\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are the semi-major and semi-minor axes of the elliptical cross-section, and \(h\) is the height. This yields
    \begin{equation*} V = \pi (0.25 \, \text{m})(0.1 \, \text{m})(1.75 \, \text{m}) \approx 0.14 \, \text{m}^3 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Knowing that the density of bronze is approximately \(8,800 \, \text{kg/m}^3\text{,}\) we can estimate the mass of the statue as
    \begin{equation*} m = \rho V = (8,800 \, \text{kg/m}^3)(0.14 \, \text{m}^3) \approx 1,200 \, \text{kg}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Hmm... this doesn’t seem right.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 1.3.2. A Better Estimate.

    Dave frowned. "Wait. If it’s 1,600 kg, the van will snap. But wait — is the statue solid or hollow?" Sarah checked the photos. "It’s cast bronze. Usually, those are hollow shells about 1 cm thick."
    🔗
    Using this new information, what would Dave’s new estimate for the mass of the statue be?
    🔗
    Solution.
    We can model the statue as a hollow elliptic cylinder, which has a volume given by the difference between the outer and inner volumes:
    \begin{equation*} V = \pi (a_o b_o h - a_i b_i h) = \pi h (a_o b_o - a_i b_i)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(a_o\) and \(b_o\) are the dimensions of the outer ellipse, and \(a_i\) and \(b_i\) are the dimensions of the inner ellipse. Since the thickness of the bronze is 1 cm, we have \(a_i = a_o - 0.01 \, \text{m}\) and \(b_i = b_o - 0.01 \, \text{m}\text{.}\) Plugging in the numbers, we get
    \begin{equation*} V = \pi (1.75 \, \text{m}) [(0.25 \, \text{m})(0.1 \, \text{m}) - (0.24 \, \text{m})(0.09 \, \text{m})] \approx 0.02 \, \text{m}^3\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This gives us an estimate for the mass of the statue of
    \begin{equation*} m = \rho V = (8,800 \, \text{kg/m}^3)(0.02 \, \text{m}^3) \approx 176 \, \text{kg}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Dave sighed with relief. "Okay, so the statue is likely between 150 kg and 600 kg depending on the internal bracing. Our 500 kg estimate is a safe upper bound for the mission."
