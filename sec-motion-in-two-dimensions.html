    Section 2.2 Motion in Two Dimensions

    Dave’s quick thinking (and our convenient constant acceleration assumption) saved the raccoons. But the screeching tires echoed across the quad, and now, flashing yellow lights appear in the rearview mirror. It is the Campus Security Night Patrol, riding in a heavily modified, gas-powered golf cart.
    🔗
    Dave floors it again, keeping the minivan moving strictly in one dimension down the perfectly straight, infinitely long campus perimeter road. But the security guards are not playing by 1D rules. The guard in the passenger seat of the golf cart reaches into a crate of stolen dining hall fruit, winds up, and hurls a lemon at the minivan.
    🔗
    We have officially entered two dimensions. Domain expansion. To figure out if Dave’s minivan is about to get juiced, we need to understand projectile motion.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.1 Introducing the Vector

    Up until now, our velocity has just been a number with a plus or minus sign, because Dave could only go forward or backward. But the guard didn’t throw the lemon straight forward or straight up. He threw it at an angle.
    🔗
    Our coordinate system is two-dimensional. This means that there are two directions that we can move in, and thus, we need two numbers to specify the position of an object. Importantly, the horizontal motion and the vertical motion are completely independent. They happen at the same time, but they do not affect one another.
    🔗
    This means that we can assign two separate functions to describe the horizontal and vertical motion of the lemon. We can call these functions \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\text{,}\) and they will give us the horizontal and vertical position of the lemon at any time \(t\text{.}\) A combination of these will trace out a curve in the \(xy\)-plane, which is the path of the lemon. The equations that trace out this curve are called the parametric equations of the curve.
    🔗
    Let’s suppose we draw an arrow from the origin to the position of the lemon at any time \(t\text{.}\) This arrow is called the position vector of the lemon, and it is denoted by \(\vb{r}(t)\text{.}\) It is analogous to the position number we had in one dimension, but now its direction is important, so it is a vector — a quantity that has both magnitude and direction (for now).
    🔗
    Suppose, for a moment, the lemon is in the air at some time \(t\text{.}\) If you were able to freeze time and look at the lemon, you would be able to measure the components of the position vector, \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\text{.}\) Remember the meter sticks in the van? You could use those to measure how far the lemon is from the origin in the horizontal and vertical directions. In fact, for convenience, let the meter stick in the \(x\) direction be a vector, say \(\vu{x}\text{,}\) that points in the positive \(x\) direction and has a length of one meter. Similarly, let the meter stick in the \(y\) direction be a vector, say \(\vu{y}\text{,}\) that points in the positive \(y\) direction and has a length of one meter. Then, we can write the position vector as a linear combination of these two unit vectors:
    \begin{equation*} \vb{r}(t) = x(t)\vu{x} + y(t)\vu{y}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    When we visualize the position vector, we can draw a curve in the \(xy\)-plane that traces out the path of the lemon. Note that this is NOT the same as the \(xt\)-graph that we used in one dimension, although both graphs seem to be two-dimensional. On the \(xt\)-graph, the horizontal axis is time, and the vertical axis is position. This means that as we move to the right, we are moving forward in time. On the \(xy\)-graph, the horizontal axis is the horizontal position, and the vertical axis is the vertical position. This means that as we move to the right, we are moving to the right in space, not forward in time.
    🔗
    Indeed, on the \(xy\)-graph, the curve is a physical thing that exists in space. Time is not explicitly represented on the graph, but it is implicitly represented by the fact that as time passes, the position vector traces out the curve. In other words, you would have to magically play a video of the lemon’s motion in order to see the motion of the lemon on the \(xy\)-graph. Or you can drop points on the curve at regular time intervals to see how the position changes as time passes. If the points are more dense in some region of the curve, that means the lemon is moving slower in that region, and vice versa. On the \(xt\)-graph, no such magic is needed. The motion of the lemon is directly represented on the graph, and you can see how the position changes as time passes just by looking at the graph.
    🔗
    If we have a position vector, there must be a velocity vector and an acceleration vector. How do we differentiate a vector-valued function? Since the horizontal and vertical motions are independent, we can just differentiate the components separately:
    \begin{equation*} \dv{\vb{r}}{t} = \dv{x}{t}\vu{x} + \dv{y}{t}\vu{y} = v_x(t)\vu{x} + v_y(t)\vu{y} = \vb{v}(t), \end{equation*}
    where \(v_x(t)\) and \(v_y(t)\) are the horizontal and vertical components of the velocity. We can now define the instantaneous velocity and acceleration of the lemon.
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.1. Instantaneous Velocity.

    The instantaneous velocity of an object at a given time is the limit of the average velocity as the time interval approaches zero:
    \begin{equation*} \vb{v}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\vb{r}(t + \Delta t) - \vb{r}(t)}{\Delta t} = \dv{\vb{r}}{t} = \dot{\vb{r}}(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.2. Instantaneous Acceleration.

    The instantaneous acceleration of an object at a given time is the limit of the average acceleration as the time interval approaches zero:
    \begin{equation*} \vb{a}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\vb{v}(t + \Delta t) - \vb{v}(t)}{\Delta t} = \dv{\vb{v}}{t} = \ddot{\vb{r}}(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    It may prove insightful to consider how we visualize these derivatives. For a scalar function, the derivative at a point is the slope of the tangent line to the curve at that point. We can’t use the same idea for a vector-valued function. On one hand, we can still decompose the position vector into its components — which are scalar functions of time — and take the derivative of each component separately. Then, each component of the velocity vector is the slope of the tangent line to the corresponding component of the position vector.
    🔗
    On the other hand, if we trace out the curve of the position vector in the \(xy\)-plane, we can draw a tangent line to the curve at any point. Now, our velocity, which is a vector, is tangent to the curve at that point. Its magnitude corresponds to how fast we are moving along the curve.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.2 Projectile Motion

    Let’s return to the problem at hand: the lemon being thrown at the minivan. We want to know if the lemon will hit the minivan, which is moving along the horizontal axis.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.2.3.

    Suppose the lemon is thrown from the origin at time \(t = 0\) with an initial speed of \(v_0\) at an angle of \(\theta\) above the horizontal. What are the components of the initial velocity vector, \(v_x(0)\) and \(v_y(0)\text{?}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The components are
    \begin{align*} v_x(0) \amp = v_0\cos\theta\\ v_y(0) \amp = v_0\sin\theta\text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Now that we have the initial velocity, we also need to know the acceleration of the lemon in order to determine its trajectory. The only force acting on the lemon is gravity, which acts downward with a magnitude of \(g\text{.}\) This means that the acceleration of the lemon is
    \begin{equation*} \vb{a}(t) = 0\vu{x} - g\vu{y}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Its components are thus
    \begin{align*} a_x(t) \amp = 0\\ a_y(t) \amp = -g\text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    We can now use the initial velocity and the acceleration to find the velocity and position of the lemon at any time \(t\text{.}\) Since the horizontal and vertical motions are independent, we can treat them separately, the same way we did in one dimension. The horizontal motion is simple: since there is no acceleration, the horizontal velocity is constant (so the same as the initial horizontal velocity),
    \begin{equation*} v_x(t) = v_0\cos\theta, \end{equation*}
    and the horizontal position is
    \begin{equation*} x(t) = v_0\cos(\theta) t. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The vertical motion is also simple, we just use Theorem 2.1.14 with the appropriate initial conditions:
    \begin{align*} v_y(t) \amp = v_0\sin\theta - gt\\ y(t) \amp = v_0\sin(\theta) t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    In vector form, the trajectory of the lemon is given by
    \begin{equation*} \vb{r}(t) = v_0\cos(\theta) t \vu{x} + \qty(v_0\sin(\theta) t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2) \vu{y}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This equation describes the parabolic trajectory of most projectiles near the surface of the Earth, as long as we can ignore air resistance and other forces.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.3 Will the Lemon Hit?

    Suppose the minivan is located at a horizontal distance \(d = 20\) meters from the origin, and a vertical height \(h = 0\) meters from the release point of the lemon. We want to know if there is an angle \(\theta\) such that the lemon will go through the window of the minivan. In other words, we want to know if there is a solution to the equation
    \begin{equation*} \vb{r}(t) = d\vu{x} + h\vu{y}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    To solve this equation, we can equate the components of the position vector to the components of the target position, giving us the system of equations
    \begin{align*} v_0\cos(\theta) t \amp = d\\ v_0\sin(\theta) t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \amp = h\text{.} \end{align*}
    Notice: our problem is purely geometric, and the time variable is just a parameter that we can eliminate. As such, we can solve for \(t\) in the first equation;
    \begin{equation*} t = \frac{d}{v_0\cos\theta}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and substitute this into the second equation to get an equation for \(\theta\text{.}\)
    🔗
    This only has real solutions if
    \begin{equation*} v_0^4 - 2ghv_0^2 - g^2d^2 \geq 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    To see the derivation, consult Exercise Group 2.4.2–4 in the exercises section.
    🔗
    We know that the minivan is located at \(d = 20\) meters and \(h = 0\) meters, so the condition for the lemon to hit the minivan is
    \begin{equation*} v_0 \geq 14\, \text{m/s}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which is about 31 miles per hour. If the guard can throw the lemon at that speed, then there will be two angles at which the lemon will hit the minivan.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.4 Derivative Rules

    Before proceeding, let’s take a moment to derive some useful rules for differentiating vector-valued functions.
    🔗
    As the security guards, riding in the golf cart, are throwing lemons frantically at us, one of the lemons got snagged by a tree branch, which caused the lemon to swing back and forth like a pendulum. The motion of the lemon can be described by the equation \(\theta(t) = \cos(2t)\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.2.4.

    Using first-principles, derive an expression for the angular velocity of the lemon, \(\omega(t) = \dv{t} \theta(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    You might want to use the trigonometric identity \(\cos A - \cos B = -2\sin\qty(\frac{A + B}{2}) \sin\qty(\frac{A - B}{2})\) to simplify the expression you get after plugging into the limit.
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    Using the definition of the derivative, we have
    \begin{equation*} \omega(t) = \dv{t} \theta(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\theta(t + \Delta t) - \theta(t)}{\Delta t} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\cos(2(t + \Delta t)) - \cos(2t)}{\Delta t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Using the trigonometric identity \(\cos A - \cos B = -2\sin\qty(\frac{A + B}{2}) \sin\qty(\frac{A - B}{2})\text{,}\) we can rewrite the numerator as
    \begin{align*} \cos(2(t + \Delta t)) - \cos(2t) \amp = -2\sin\qty(\frac{2(t + \Delta t) + 2t}{2}) \sin\qty(\frac{2(t + \Delta t) - 2t}{2}) \\ \amp = -2\sin(2t + \Delta t) \sin(\Delta t) \text{.} \end{align*}
    Therefore, we have
    \begin{equation*} \omega(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{-2\sin(2t + \Delta t) \sin(\Delta t)}{\Delta t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    As \(\Delta t \to 0\text{,}\) we have \(\sin(2t + \Delta t) \to \sin(2t)\) and \(\frac{\sin(\Delta t)}{\Delta t} \to 1\text{,}\) so we get
    \begin{equation*} \omega(t) = -2\sin(2t)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The above was a straightforward application of the definition of the derivative, but it was quite tedious. Indeed, if we had to find every derivative using first-principles, we would literally never get anything done. In physics, we like to at least pretend that we are doing anything other than math, so we need some shortcuts for finding derivatives.
    🔗
    Notice the pattern in the above solution: we took the derivative of an expression consisting of an outer function (in this case, the cosine function) and an inner function (in this case, the linear function of time, \(2t\)), and we ended up with the derivative of the outer function (which is \(-\sin(2t)\)) multiplied by the derivative of the inner function (which is \(2\)). Is this just a coincidence, or is there some deeper reason for this pattern? See the next two exercises for the answer.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.2.5.

    Show that more generally, if the trajectory of an object is given by \(x(t) = f(g(t))\text{,}\) then the velocity of the object is given by \(v(t) = \dv{t} f(g(t)) = \dv{f}{g} \cdot \dv{g}{t}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Using the definition of the derivative, we have
    \begin{equation*} v(t) = \dv{t} f(g(t)) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{f(g(t + \Delta t)) - f(g(t))}{\Delta t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We can add and subtract \(f(g(t + \Delta t))\) in the numerator to get
    \begin{align*} v(t) \amp = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{f(g(t + \Delta t)) - f(g(t)) + f(g(t + \Delta t)) - f(g(t))}{\Delta t} \\ \amp = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \qty[ \frac{f(g(t + \Delta t)) - f(g(t))}{g(t + \Delta t) - g(t)} \cdot \frac{g(t + \Delta t) - g(t)}{\Delta t} ] \text{.} \end{align*}
    Notice that the first fraction is the definition of the derivative of \(f\) with respect to \(g\text{,}\) and the second fraction is the definition of the derivative of \(g\) with respect to \(t\text{.}\) Therefore, as \(\Delta t \to 0\text{,}\) the first fraction approaches \(\dv{f}{g}\) and the second fraction approaches \(\dv{g}{t}\text{,}\) so we get
    \begin{equation*} v(t) = \dv{f}{g} \cdot \dv{g}{t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.2.6.

    The statement of the previous exercise is known as the chain rule, and it is one of the most important rules of differentiation. Can you explain why the chain rule makes intuitive sense?
    🔗
    Solution.
    Suppose we want to find out how much \(f\) changes when we change \(t\text{.}\) Since \(f\) changes when we change \(g\text{,}\) we know that \(f\) changes by \(\dv{f}{g} \cdot \Delta g\) when we change \(g\) by \(\Delta g\text{.}\) But since \(g\) changes when we change \(t\text{,}\) we know that \(g\) changes by \(\dv{g}{t} \cdot \Delta t\) when we change \(t\) by \(\Delta t\text{.}\) Therefore, when we change \(t\text{,}\) \(f\) changes by \(\dv{f}{g} \cdot \dv{g}{t} \cdot \Delta t\text{,}\) which means that the rate of change of \(f\) with respect to \(t\) is \(\dv{f}{g} \cdot \dv{g}{t}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The chain rule allows us to differentiate compositions of functions without having to go back to first-principles every time. What about products of functions? For example, if we have a rectangle with length \(l(t)\) and width \(w(t)\text{,}\) how do we find the rate of change of the area of the rectangle, which is given by \(A(t) = l(t) w(t)\text{?}\)
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.2.7.

    Now suppose we have a rectangle with length \(l(t)\) and width \(w(t)\text{,}\) where both are functions of time. Can you derive an expression for the rate of change of the area of the rectangle with respect to time, i.e., \(\dv{t} (l(t) w(t))\text{?}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Draw the rectangle out. If you increase both the length and the width by a small amount, how much does the area increase by?
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    If we increase the length by a small amount \(\Delta l\) and the width by a small amount \(\Delta w\text{,}\) then the area increases by
    \begin{equation*} \Delta A = l \Delta w + w \Delta l + \Delta l \Delta w\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Dividing both sides by \(\Delta t\) and taking the limit as \(\Delta t \to 0\text{,}\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \dv{A}{t} = l \dv{w}{t} + w \dv{l}{t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.5 Polar Coordinates and Uniform Circular Motion

    Fortunately, the guard is not a professional athlete, and the lemon is not going to hit the minivan. The lemon splatters harmlessly against the pavement behind them. Dave has successfully navigated the 1D perimeter road, but the flashing lights of the security golf cart are still in the rearview mirror.
    🔗
    "Hang on!" Dave yells. He yanks the steering wheel to the left, and the minivan screeches into the infamous "Founder’s Quad Traffic Circle" — a massive, perfectly circular roundabout that has claimed the hubcaps of countless freshmen. Inside the van, absolute chaos ensues. Even though Dave is keeping the speedometer pegged at a constant 15 m/s, everyone is violently shoved against the right-hand doors. In the back, the 500 kg bronze statue of Richard Feynman groans and begins to slide across the carpet.
    🔗
    Wait a minute. If Dave’s speed is constant, why is everyone feeling an acceleration? The short answer is that the direction of the velocity is changing, even though its magnitude is not. For a fuller understanding, we need to introduce a new coordinate system that is more natural for circular motion: polar coordinates.
    🔗
    In our usual (Cartesian) coordinate system, we specify the position of an object by giving its horizontal and vertical coordinates, \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) We don’t have to forsake Cartesian coordinates to describe circular motion. We could describe the van using a messy conglomeration of sines and cosines. But think about Dave’s perspective. When he looks out the windshield, he doesn’t care about his \(x\)-coordinate. He only cares about two things: "Am I going forward?" and "Am I turning the wheel?" So, let’s invent a coordinate system that rides inside the minivan with Dave.
    🔗
    A coordinate system specifies (1) the origin point, (2) the directions of the axes, (3) which direction is positive, and (4) unit vectors that point in the positive directions of the axes. The origin remains the same in polar coordinates, but the unit vectors are different. The tangential vector, \(\vu{\theta}\text{,}\) points in the direction of motion, and the radial vector, \(\vu{r}\text{,}\) points from the origin to the position of the object. The tangential vector is positive in the direction of motion, and the radial vector is positive in the outward direction.
    🔗
    In Cartesian coordinates, \(\vu{x}\) and \(\vu{y}\) are constant unit vectors that point in the same direction at all times. In polar coordinates, \(\vu{\theta}\) and \(\vu{r}\) are not constant. Their directions change as the object moves around the circle.
    🔗
    At a given point, the polar unit vectors can be expressed in terms of the Cartesian unit vectors as
    \begin{align*} \vu{r} \amp = \cos\theta \vu{x} + \sin\theta \vu{y}\\ \vu{\theta} \amp = -\sin\theta \vu{x} + \cos\theta \vu{y}\text{.} \end{align*}
    From this, it is clear that they depend on the position (specifically, the angle \(\theta\)). The Cartesian unit vectors can also be expressed in terms of the polar unit vectors as
    \begin{align*} \vu{x} \amp = \cos\theta \vu{r} - \sin\theta \vu{\theta}\\ \vu{y} \amp = \sin\theta \vu{r} + \cos\theta \vu{\theta}\text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    If the polar unit vectors depend on the position, then they also depend on time, since the position depends on time. The natural question is, then, what are the derivatives of the polar unit vectors?
    🔗

    Theorem 2.2.8.

    The derivatives of the polar unit vectors are given by
    \begin{align*} \dv{t} \vu{r} \amp = \dv{\theta}{t} \vu{\theta}\\ \dv{t} \vu{\theta} \amp = -\dv{\theta}{t} \vu{r}\text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Here I will present a more visual derivation. A more rigorous derivation is in the exercises section.
    🔗
    🔗
    As we speak, the minivan is moving around the traffic circle at a constant speed of 15 m/s. Let’s begin by stating the position of the van in polar coordinates:
    \begin{equation*} \vb{r}(t) = R\vu{r}(t)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(R\) is the radius of the traffic circle, and \(\vu{r}(t)\) is the radial unit vector that points from the center of the circle to the position of the van at time \(t\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The velocity of the van is the derivative of the position:
    \begin{equation*} \vb{v}(t) = \dv{\vb{r}}{t} = R\dv{\vu{r}}{t} = R\dv{\theta}{t}\vu{\theta}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The velocity points in the tangential direction, which makes sense since the van is moving around the circle. The term \(\dv{\theta}{t}\) is called the angular velocity, and it is the rate at which the angle \(\theta\) changes with respect to time. It is denoted by \(\omega\) (Greek letter omega), so we can write the velocity as
    \begin{equation*} \vb{v}(t) = R\omega \vu{\theta}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Note that this is only applicable if \(R\) is constant! If the van were moving in a spiral, then \(R\) would not be constant, and we would have to use the product rule to find the velocity:
    \begin{equation*} \vb{v}(t) = \dv{R}{t} \vu{r} + R\dv{\vu{r}}{t} = \dv{R}{t} \vu{r} + R\omega \vu{\theta}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Next, we can find the acceleration of the van by taking the derivative of the velocity. For uniform circular motion, we have
    \begin{equation*} \vb{a}(t) = \dv{\vb{v}}{t} = R\dv{\omega}{t} \vu{\theta} + R\omega \dv{\vu{\theta}}{t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The first term is zero, since the angular velocity is constant ("uniform" circular motion). So
    \begin{equation*} \vb{a}(t) = R\omega \dv{\vu{\theta}}{t} = R\omega (-\omega \vu{r}) = -R\omega^2 \vu{r}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    So this is it! There is an acceleration, even though the speed is constant, because the direction of the velocity, \(\vu{\theta}\text{,}\) is changing. This is why everyone in the van is feeling an acceleration, even though the speedometer is pegged at 15 m/s. This is why the statue of Feynman is sliding across the carpet.
    🔗
    When discussing circular motion, it is very convenient to disregard the radius, and only discuss the angular displacement \(\theta\text{,}\) the angular velocity \(\omega\text{,}\) and the angular acceleration \(\alpha = \dv{\omega}{t}\text{.}\) Mathematically speaking, they are very similar to their linear counterparts, but they have different units and different physical interpretations. In fact, under constant angular acceleration, the equations of motion for angular displacement, velocity, and acceleration are exactly the same:
    \begin{align*} \omega \amp = \omega_0 + \alpha t\\ \Delta \theta \amp = \omega_0 t + \frac{1}{2} \alpha t^2\\ \omega^2 \amp = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha \Delta \theta\\ \Delta \theta \amp = \frac{\omega_0 + \omega}{2} t \end{align*}
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.2.9.

    The van continues to move around the traffic circle at a constant speed of 15 m/s. The traffic circle has a radius of 15 meters. How long does it take for the van to complete one full revolution around the circle? This quantity is called the period of the motion, and it is denoted by \(T\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The angular velocity of the van is given by \(\omega = \frac{v}{R} = \frac{15\, \text{ m/s}}{15\, \text{ m}} = 1\, \text{ rad/s}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To complete one full revolution, the van needs to go through an angular displacement of \(2\pi\) radians. Since the angular velocity is constant, we can use the equation \(\Delta \theta = \omega t\) to find the time it takes to complete one full revolution. Substituting in the values, we get
    \begin{equation*} 2\pi = 1 \cdot T \implies T = 2\pi\, \text{seconds}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Following the exercise above, we can define a few quantities that are commonly used in circular motion.
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.10. Quantities in Circular Motion.

    The period of a motion is the time it takes to complete one full cycle of the motion. It is denoted by
    \begin{equation*} T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The frequency of a motion is the number of cycles completed per unit time,
    \begin{equation*} f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{\omega}{2\pi}\text{.} \end{equation*}
